About me:

I am an isotope geochemist who seeks to understand the co-evolution of interlinked cryospheric, geologic, biologic, and oceanographic systems. My research centers on the the application of geochemical tools to extend our record of Antarctic Ice Sheet change into the recent geologic past using presently ice covered archives.

I am currently (beginning February, 2021) a postdoctoral fellow at Tulane University working to understand the recent history of the Thwaites-Pine Island glacier system as part of the International Thwaites Glacier Collaboration. I recently completed my Ph.D. at the University of South Florida (January, 2021) where I worked to reconstruct Holocene grounding line history in the southern Ross Sea Embayment as part of the Subglacial Antarctic Lakes Scientific Access (SALSA) Project.

In the field

I participate in field work on ice, at sea, and over land to collect samples and environmental data for my research

  • on ice

    As part of the Subglacial Antarctic Lakes Scientific Access (SALSA) Project, I have spent two field seasons in Antarctica. In 2018-2019, we drilled through ~1100 meters of ice into Mercer Subglacial Lake to collect sediment and water samples.

  • at sea

    I have participated in two research cruises (Spring 2013 & 2014) on the R/V Robert Gordon Sproul. We collected sediment samples off the coast of San Diego to investigate living benthic foraminiferal populations.

  • over land

    The Western Cape of South Africa hosts geology that reflects the break up and formation of Gondwana, as well as Saharan and Gondwanan glaciations. I have spent two summers (2011 as a student, 2013 as a teaching assistant) in South Africa mapping geology.

Community Outreach & Education

"Antarctica and the Southern Ocean offer great opportunities for inspiring popular interest in science in much the same way as space exploration did in the latter half of the 20th century."
-From the 2015 NAS Strategic Vision for NSF Investments in Antarctic and Southern Ocean Research

I am passionate about accessing, fostering, and amplifying diverse voices in higher education. I believe that exposure to cutting-edge Antarctic research early in a student's education has the ability to catalyze their interest in STEM. I actively participate in community-based education by creating hands-on activities based on my research for K-12 students. My labmates and I have used these activities in local St. Petersburg classrooms and after-school programs to teach students about sediment coring for paleoclimate research, the exploration of environments under ice, ocean circulation, sea level rise, and Antarctic Ice Sheet change.
