About me:

I am an isotope geochemist who seeks to understand the co-evolution of interlinked cryospheric, geologic, biologic, and oceanographic systems. My research centers on the the application of geochemical tools to extend our record of Antarctic Ice Sheet change into the geologic past.
I measure the isotopic composition (Δ¹⁴C, δ¹³C, δ¹⁵N) of sub-ice (subglacial and grounding-line-proximal) sediment and water samples to explore the origin, age, and cycling of carbon beneath the West Antarctic Ice Sheet. My research helps to constrain past grounding line behavior so that we may place modern ice sheet change into the context of long-term variability.
I am currently a Ph.D. candidate at the University of South Florida's College of Marine Science. I hold a B.S. in Geology from Indiana University Northwest (2013) and a M.S. from Indiana State University in Earth and Quaternary systems (2015).

In the field

I participate in field work on ice, at sea, and over land to collect samples and environmental data for my research

  • on ice

    As part of the Subglacial Antarctic Lakes Scientific Access (SALSA) Project, I have spent two field seasons in Antarctica. In 2018-2019, we drilled through ~1100 meters of ice into Mercer Subglacial Lake to collect sediment and water samples.

  • at sea

    I have participated in two research cruises (Spring 2013 & 2014) on the R/V Robert Gordon Sproul. We collected sediment samples off the coast of San Diego to investigate living benthic foraminiferal populations.

  • over land

    The Western Cape of South Africa hosts geology that reflects the break up and formation of Gondwana, as well as Saharan and Gondwanan glaciations. I have spent two summers (2011 as a student, 2013 as a teaching assistant) in South Africa mapping geology.

Community Outreach & Education

"Antarctica and the Southern Ocean offer great opportunities for inspiring popular interest in science in much the same way as space exploration did in the latter half of the 20th century."
-From the 2015 NAS Strategic Vision for NSF Investments in Antarctic and Southern Ocean Research

The allure of Antarctic research provides a unique opportunity to communicate science to an audience of all ages. I participate in community-based education by giving presentations about my research in St. Petersburg area K-12 classrooms and after school programs. I have created hands-on activities for community members of all ages centered on the use of sediment cores in paleoclimate research, the exploration of environments under ice, and ocean circulation, and glacier movement. As a first-generation college student myself, I recognize that early exposure to Antarctic research has the ability to attract a diverse next generation of STEM students.
